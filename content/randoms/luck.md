## First computer

It was 2012, and my school got its first computer lab. I remember staring in awe
at Google Earth, jumping across the world to random locations and gawking at the
3D blobs. Not much came of it, but at 7, typing class was already my favorite.

## First taste of programming

For whatever reason, at around 8, my parents signed me up for a robotics
program. There, I built little machines out of Lego with motors and a compute
unit the size of a rat. We'd then insert magnetic cards with pre-programmed
actions to control its actions. There was no code written, but the order of
inserted cards allowed the robot to complete a range of tasks.

![Scratch logo](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Scratchlogo.svg/500px-Scratchlogo.svg.png)
A year later, I learned Scratch to make games at another kids center. Despite
being a block-based/visual and not a "real" programming language, understanding
the concept of event loops and control flow, even without knowing the
vocabulary, helped immensely later on in "real programming".

## Living with an American host family

Accents influence how smart someone sounds, with the perception largely based on
social and cultural biases.

At 10 years old, my parents decided to move to China for better opportunities,
while I decided to stay in Malaysia. I was too young to be allowed into boarding
school, and as a compromise, I ended up staying with an American teacher's
family for a year while studying at an American missionary school in Penang.

One of the things I noticed is that even if you are taught in English, as long
as friends and family aren't native speakers, kids tend to adopt the accent and
vocabulary of their social circle rather than teachers.

Being a missionary school, the demographics were much more skewed towards native
speakers, with around 50% American, 30% Korean, and 20% Malaysian. With my
entire social circle being native English speakers, I lost my Malaysian accent
by the end of the year.

After the year was over, my parents decided to move back to Malaysia.

During the 2024 Huawei Tech Arena, simply being more articulate than the teams
with better technical implementations scored our team a 2nd place out of 200
while the #1 dropped down to 4th place due to a botched presentation.
